00
Before anything else

Read This First

You're reading this because a clear-headed version of you knew a foggy version might come back. Listen to the clear one.

If you feel the pull to go back — stop here and breathe.

The pull is real. But real is not the same as true. Research on traumatic bonding identifies two conditions that built this pull: a power imbalance that narrowed your options, and intermittent reinforcement that trained your nervous system to chase relief from the same source as the pain. What feels like love right now is often those two things working as designed — not a signal to go back.

You did not leave because you were confused. You are confused now because you left. That's the cycle working exactly as it was designed to — and it does not mean you were wrong to go.

You don't have to decide anything this moment. You just have to not act yet. Read on. Then wait.

01
Keep this where you'll find it

The Card

If you only have ten seconds, this is the part to keep. Screenshot it. Tape it somewhere you'll see it.

When the pull comes, before I respond:

  1. I will not reply for at least 48 hours. Nothing real falls apart in 48 hours.
  2. I will call or text one safe person before I contact them.
  3. I will re-read my own reasons for leaving — further down in this guide.
  4. I will remember: the warmth always came back, right before it vanished again. That pattern was the mechanism — not the exception.
  5. This feeling passes whether or not I go back. Going back is the only choice that can't be undone.
02
The return — "hoovering"

Is It Happening Again?

The pull back in rarely looks like danger. It looks like hope. Tap each one that's happening — seeing them counted can cut through the fog.

  • A sweeping apologyThe words you always wanted — with no real change behind them.
  • "I've changed / I'm getting help now"Possible — some people do change through sustained voluntary work. But real change shows up over months of action, not in a message sent the week you left. What you're feeling right now is Phase 1, not evidence of change.
  • A sudden crisis or emergencyThey're sick, in trouble, "only you can help." Manufactured need pulls on your compassion deliberately.
  • "Remember when…"Flooding you with Phase 1 memories — the version of them who rarely stayed.
  • Messages through other peopleFriends, family, or children carrying word that they miss you or aren't doing well.
  • Perfect timingContact landing on a holiday, anniversary, or hard day they knew about — when you're most likely to be lonely.
  • "Just one conversation"A request so small it feels unreasonable to refuse. The door only needs to open a crack.
  • Pity, threats, or guilt"I can't live without you." "After everything I did." Different tactics, same goal: reopen the door.
  • Love-bombing, round twoGifts, intensity, the very beginning again. It felt exactly like this the first time too.
  • Showing up uninvitedAt your door, work, or usual places. (If this frightens you, skip ahead to Safety.)
03
Naming the fog

The Feeling Is Not The Fact

When the longing hits, your mind hands you an explanation pointing back toward them. Here's what's usually on the other side.

Feels like

"I miss them so much it hurts. That must mean it was love."

Likely true

Missing someone and being safe with them are two different things. Withdrawal hurts. The ache is real; it isn't evidence.

Feels like

"Maybe I overreacted. Maybe it wasn't that bad."

Likely true

Minimising is how you survived it. Distance is letting the fog clear — not exaggerating. Trust what you knew when you were clear.

Feels like

"They seem so different now. Maybe they really have changed."

Likely true

You've seen this warmth before — it's Phase 1. Real change is measured in months of consistent action, not a moment of charm sent your way.

Feels like

"No one else will ever understand me like they did."

Likely true

That belief was built through systematic isolation — a documented tactic that maintained the power imbalance. It is not a fact about your future.

Feels like

"If I just explain it right this time, they'll finally understand."

Likely true

You've explained it many times. The problem was never your words. One more conversation is the hook, not the solution.

You are not going back to the person who hurt you. You're going back to the hope of the person they pretended to be. That person was never available to stay.— The Anchor

05
A plan made in advance

If They Reach Out

Decide now what you'll do, so you don't have to decide in the middle of the flood. This is the plan.

Don't respond right away

The urge to reply instantly is the pull, not you. Put the phone down. Nothing genuine is lost by waiting 48 hours.

Tell one safe person first

Before you do anything else: "They reached out. I'm not okay right now." Saying it out loud breaks the secrecy the cycle depends on.

Re-read your own reasons

Scroll up to what you wrote in My Reasons. That's the clear-headed you, talking to the you who's wavering right now. Believe that one.

Ask the harder question

Not "do I miss them?" — you will. Ask: if nothing about them has actually changed, what would going back cost me? You already know the answer.

For unavoidable contact, stay flat

Co-parenting, shared logistics: keep it short, factual, unemotional. No feelings to feed on, no opening to widen. Boring is protection. Document everything in writing.

Let the wave pass

The intensity peaks and then falls, every time — usually within hours. You don't have to fight it forever. You just have to outlast this one wave.

06
This part matters most

Staying Safe

The time around leaving can be the most dangerous. Planning ahead is not paranoia — it is wisdom.

Practical steps

  • Trust escalation as a signal. If contact turns to anger, threats, or showing up uninvited — that is information. Take it seriously even when part of you wants to minimise it.
  • Keep a record. Save threatening messages, log unwanted contact with dates and details. You don't have to act now, but documentation protects you later.
  • Tell people what's happening. A few trusted people knowing your situation is a safety net at home, at work, wherever you spend time.
  • Know your exits. If you share space or must meet, think through how you'd leave and where you'd go. Keep essentials — keys, phone, ID, a little cash — reachable.
  • A hotline is not only for emergencies. Advocates help with safety planning and thinking through options, free and confidential — before anything escalates.
  • If you are ever in immediate danger, call 911. Your safety comes before any plan on this page.

Who you call instead

My People

When the pull says "call them," this is who you call instead. Fill this in now, so the names are ready before you need them.

Aim for at least two or three — one who calms you down, one who'll come over, one who'll tell you the truth. You don't have to carry this alone.
