Part One
Understanding What Happened
You can't trust-fall out of a trap you can't see. First, the shape of it.
01
The four phases

The Cycle

Four phases. Repeating. Each loop tightens the bond and makes the last one feel further away than it was.

A note on language before we begin

This guide uses "narcissistic abuse" because that's how many people first find their experience named. But know this: "narcissistic abuse" is not a clinical diagnosis, and most people who abuse do not have a diagnosable personality disorder. Abuse is a pattern of coercive control — a series of choices. You do not need to diagnose anyone to recognise harm and act on it.

Phase 1

Idealize

Love Bombing

Overwhelming affection and praise. They reflect your best qualities back — you feel profoundly seen. That feeling is real. What isn't real is that they caused it. You were, in part, falling for your own goodness reflected back.

"I've never felt this understood." — You're experiencing your own light shown back to you.
Phase 2

Devalue

Criticism, Cold, Contempt

Warmth is withdrawn without warning. Criticism begins, then escalates. You ask what you did wrong. The answer is nothing — but the question itself is the point. You're now doing double work: managing your pain and theirs.

"What did I do? I've been trying so hard." — The confusion is the mechanism, not a side effect.
Phase 3

Discard

Withdrawal, Abandonment

They leave or go silent. The threat of abandonment activates your deepest survival wiring — the brain treats it like physical danger. Not weakness: neurobiology doing exactly what it was built to do.

"I can't breathe without them." — What feels like love here is a nervous system in alarm.
Phase 4

Hoover

The Return

They come back — apologies, promises, the person you first met. Your brain's reward, bonding, and stress systems shift at once, overlapping with the brain's addiction pathways. He is not choosing you. He is choosing what you provide. The cycle restarts, deeper than before.

"This is the real them. It'll be different now." — It felt exactly like this last time too. (Dutton & Painter, 1993)

Two necessary conditions produce trauma bonding: a power imbalance (financial, social, emotional, or physical control) and intermittent reinforcement (unpredictable alternation of warmth and harm). Both must be present. Together they build a dependency that logic alone rarely breaks. (Dutton & Painter, 1981; 1993)

02
The engine

Why It Happens

Understanding the source doesn't excuse it — it removes you from the centre of it.

One explanation, not the only one

The "self-hatred behind the mask" picture fits some abusive people well. It is a useful lens, not a settled fact, and does not describe every abuser. Some who harm feel little inner conflict. Either way: the cause lives in them, not in you.

Why are they so cruel?
In the vulnerable narcissism pattern, shame mediates the link to aggression — self-hatred is directed outward. In the grandiose pattern, cruelty can be more deliberate. Either way: the cruelty originated in them, and was never earned by you.
Why build you up, then tear you down?
When you reflect their idealised self back, they're warm. When you show something true, they can't bear it. They attack the mirror, not the woman.
Why do they keep coming back?
Not for you — for what you provide. They come back for the supply, not the person. They may not know the difference.
Why is nothing ever enough?
Because the problem was never your performance. You were never the cause. You could never be the cure.
Part Two
The Blame, The Guilt & Your Own Struggles
If you've been told you're the real problem — the drinker, the unstable one, the reason it all happens — this part is for you.
03
"But maybe I really am the problem"

When You're Carrying Your Own Struggles

Drinking. Using. Depression. Anger that scares you. If you have struggles of your own, you may have been told they are the reason for everything. Let's look at that honestly — and fairly.

Two things can be real at the same time. You can have a genuine problem with alcohol or drugs — one that deserves real care — and be the target of abuse that you did nothing to earn. Neither cancels the other. Neither is permission for the other.

An abusive partner needs you to believe these two facts are the same fact. They need "you drink" to mean "so this is your fault." It does not. A drinking problem is something to heal. It is never a reason to be controlled, frightened, or harmed.

True: you may have a real problem

Your substance use, your depression, your reactions — these can be real and worth addressing with honest, compassionate help. Naming them isn't agreeing with your abuser; it's taking your own life back.

— and at the same time —

True: it does not justify the abuse

Nothing about your struggles makes you deserve fear, control, or harm. There is no amount of drinking that earns abuse. The harm was a choice someone else made — every single time.

"I wasn't a perfect partner" is true of every human being alive. It is not the same as "I caused the abuse." Hold the first with honesty. Put the second down — it was never yours.

They didn't hurt you because you drank. Many people drink and are never harmed for it. They hurt you because they chose to — and your struggle just gave them a story that let them keep choosing it.
04
Substance-use coercion & DARVO

How Your Struggle Gets Weaponized

This has a name. Researchers call it "substance-use coercion" — a recognised tactic of control. Once you see the moves, they lose some of their grip.

You're a drunk — no one will ever believe you.
What's really happening

This is about credibility. By making your struggle the headline, they ensure your word can be dismissed if you ever reach out — to police, family, or a court. Undermining your believability is a documented control tactic, not an accident.

Look what you made me do. If you weren't like this, I wouldn't have to.
What's really happening

This is DARVO — Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim & Offender. They deny the harm, attack your stability, and flip the roles so you become the offender. Research links DARVO directly to increased self-blame in the person being harmed. The guilt you feel is the tactic working.

Here, have another. Let's just drink and forget the fight.
What's really happening

Encouraging your use keeps you easier to control — less able to plan, remember clearly, or leave. Studies report that a majority of abusers actively discourage or sabotage attempts to get sober, because your dependence serves them.

I'll tell them you're an unfit mother. They'll take the kids.
What's really happening

Threats to use your struggles against you in custody or with authorities are designed to trap you in place. They are frightening precisely because they hold a grain of plausibility — which is what makes documentation and an advocate so important (see Part Four).

If you ever lashed back

Many people who are cornered eventually shout, throw something, or hit back — then are told they are the abuser. Clinicians increasingly call this a reactive defence, not abuse: a survival response to sustained provocation. A single defensive reaction inside a long pattern of control is not the same as the pattern itself.

05
Why the drinking grew

The Pain Loop — and How It Breaks

If your use got worse during the relationship, there's a reason that has nothing to do with weakness.

1

The harm — fear, humiliation, chronic stress with no safe outlet.

↓
2

The numbing — a drink quiets the alarm. For a moment, you can breathe. (Research calls this self-medication; trauma usually comes first, the substance after.)

↓
3

The blame — "See? You're the drunk. This is you." The use becomes their evidence, and your shame.

↓
4

More harm, more shame — which makes the next drink make more sense. The loop tightens.

Because the trauma and the substance are linked, treating them together works. The current standard of care is integrated, trauma-informed treatment — addressing the pain and the substance use as one connected thing. You don't have to "get clean first to deserve help with the abuse," or vice versa. Both, together, is what the evidence supports.

You are not a bad person with a drinking problem. You may be a hurt person who found the only relief that was available. That reframe isn't an excuse — it's the doorway to the right kind of help.

Getting honest about your own struggles and refusing to carry the blame for someone else's cruelty are not opposites. They're the same act of self-respect.
Part Three
Leaving & Staying Gone
Knowing the cycle isn't enough — because when the pull comes, clarity vanishes. This part is for that moment.
06
Before anything else

When The Pull Comes Back

You're reading this because a clear-headed version of you knew a foggy version might return. Listen to the clear one.

If you feel the pull to go back — stop here and breathe.

The pull is real. But real is not the same as true. Research on traumatic bonding identifies two conditions that created this pull: a power imbalance and intermittent reinforcement. What feels like love right now is often those two things working as designed.

You did not leave because you were confused. You're confused now because you left. That's the cycle doing what it was built to do — not proof you were wrong.

You don't have to decide anything this moment. You just have to not act yet. Read on. Then wait.

07
Keep this where you'll find it

The Card

If you only have ten seconds, keep this. Screenshot it.

When the pull comes, before I respond:

  1. I won't reply for at least 48 hours. Nothing real falls apart in 48 hours.
  2. I'll contact one safe person before I contact them.
  3. I'll re-read my own reasons for leaving.
  4. I'll remember: the warmth always came back right before it vanished again.
  5. This feeling passes whether or not I go back. Going back is the only choice that can't be undone.
  6. My drinking or struggle is not a reason to return. Their story about it was never the truth.
08
The return — "hoovering"

Is It Happening Again?

The pull back in rarely looks like danger. It looks like hope. Tap each one that's happening.

  • A sweeping apologyThe words you always wanted — with no real change behind them.
  • "I've changed / I'm getting help now"Real change shows up over months of action, not in a message sent the week you left.
  • A sudden crisisThey're sick, in trouble, "only you can help." Manufactured need pulls on your compassion.
  • "Remember when"Flooding you with Phase 1 memories — the version of them that rarely stayed.
  • Messages through other peopleFriends, family, or children carrying their message.
  • Perfect timingContact landing on a holiday, anniversary, or hard day they knew about.
  • Using your struggle as a hook"You'll relapse without me." "You can't cope alone."
  • Pity, threats, or guilt"I can't live without you." "After everything I did." Different tactics, same goal.
  • Love-bombing, round twoGifts, intensity, the very beginning again. It felt like that the first time too.
  • Showing up uninvitedAt your door, work, your usual places. (If this frightens you, go to Part Four — Safety.)
09
Naming the fog

The Feeling Is Not The Fact

Feels like

"I miss them so much it hurts. That must mean it was love."

Likely true

Missing and being safe with someone are different things. Withdrawal hurts. The ache is real; it isn't evidence.

Feels like

"Maybe I overreacted. Maybe it wasn't that bad."

Likely true

Minimising is how you survived. Distance is letting the fog clear. Trust what you knew when you were clear.

Feels like

"They were right — I'm a mess. Who else would put up with me?"

Likely true

That belief was constructed through systematic isolation — a documented tactic. It is not a fact about your future.

Feels like

"If I just explain it right this time, they'll finally get it."

Likely true

You've explained it many times. The problem was never your words. One more conversation is the hook, not the solution.

You're not going back to the person who hurt you. You're going back to the hope of the person they pretended to be. That person was never available to stay.

11
A plan made in advance

If They Reach Out

Don't respond right away

Nothing genuine is lost by waiting. Put the phone down. 48 hours is a good floor.

Tell one safe person first

"They reached out. I'm not okay right now." Saying it out loud breaks the secrecy the cycle depends on.

Re-read your own reasons

The clear-headed you, talking to the you that's wavering. Believe that one.

Ask the harder question

Not "do I miss them?" — you will. Ask: if nothing has actually changed, what would going back cost me?

For unavoidable contact, go grey rock

Short, factual, unemotional. No feelings to feed on, no opening to widen. Document everything in writing.

Let the wave pass

The intensity peaks and falls, usually within hours. You only have to outlast this one wave.

Part Four
Safety, Your People & Help
The most practical part. Keep this close.
12
Physical safety first

Staying Safe

The time around leaving can be the most dangerous. Planning ahead is not paranoia — it's wisdom.

Practical steps

  • Trust escalation as a signal. Anger, threats, or showing up uninvited after contact — take it seriously even when part of you wants to minimise.
  • Keep a record. Save threatening messages, log unwanted contact with dates. Documentation protects you later.
  • Tell people what's happening. A few trusted people knowing your situation is a safety net at home, at work, wherever you spend time.
  • Know your exits. Think through how you'd leave and where you'd go. Keep keys, phone, ID, and some cash reachable.
  • A hotline is not only for emergencies. Advocates help with safety planning and thinking through options — free and confidential, before anything escalates.
  • If you are ever in immediate danger, call 911. Your safety comes before any plan on this page.
