Before the checklist

Going Back Is Not An Event

It's a process. It almost never starts with a decision — it starts with a feeling, then a thought, long before any contact happens.

Nobody wakes up one morning and decides to go back.

The slide begins quietly — weeks, sometimes months, before any message is sent. First the mood shifts. Then the thinking shifts. Only last does the behaviour shift. By the time you're reaching for the phone, the current has been carrying you for a while.

That's good news. It means there are early signs — and if you can name them while you're still in the shallows, you can turn around before the water is over your head.

01
What the research shows

The Pull Has Early Signs

Researchers who study leaving abusive relationships find the same thing every time: leaving is not one clean event, and returning is not a sudden choice. Both are slow, cyclical processes — which means there are signs you can learn to read.

The back-and-forth is normal, expected, and well-documented. It is not weakness, and it is not failure. Most people who leave for good did it after returning at least once. The signs of being pulled back tend to arrive in a rough order — the mood shifts first, then the thinking, and only last the actions.

1

The mood shifts first

You're not thinking about them yet — but your self-care and connection are slipping. The easiest point to turn around, and the easiest to miss.

2

Then the thinking shifts

A debate starts in your head. The good memories get louder and the harm goes quiet — the mind plays the highlight reel. Old self-blame creeps back in.

3

Then the actions shift

Checking, re-reading, "just one conversation." This is the point closest to contact — and the moment to reach for another person hardest.

A word about blame

The pull was built from two conditions: a power imbalance that narrowed your options, and intermittent reinforcement that trained your nervous system to chase relief from the same source as the pain (Dutton & Painter, 1981; 1993). The aim of this tool is to notice the drift early. And if you do return — many people do, often more than once — that is part of how leaving usually works, not proof something is wrong with you. This is a mirror, never a scorecard.

02
Honest, private, just for you

The Self-Check

Tap anything that's true for you lately. Be honest — no one sees this but you. The meter at the bottom moves as you go.

FirstThe mood shift

Not about them yet — about you slipping.

  • I've been pulling away from my support people — cancelling, going quiet, isolating.
  • I've stopped doing the things that were helping me (therapy, exercise, sleep, journalling).
  • My sleep or eating has gotten noticeably worse, or I feel "off" in my body.
  • There's a restlessness or emptiness I can't quite name.
  • I've been drinking or using more than I meant to — to take the edge off.
  • When people ask how I'm doing, I say "fine" when I'm not.
NextThe thinking shift

The debate has started in your head.

  • I keep replaying the good times — and the bad parts feel fuzzy or far away.
  • I've caught myself thinking "maybe it wasn't as bad as I made it out to be."
  • The old self-blame is back — "if I'd just been better / hadn't been drinking, maybe…"
  • I've been telling myself "this time would be different" or "people can change."
  • I'm starting to believe no one else will ever understand me the way they did.
  • I find myself defending them or softening the story when I talk about them.
  • I'm looking for reasons it would be "okay" to make contact — the kids, closure, a birthday.
ThenThe action shift

Thoughts are turning into actions.

  • I've been checking their social media, driving past, or quietly keeping tabs.
  • I've re-read old messages, looked at old photos, or kept a "just in case" door open.
  • I responded to their contact and told myself it was nothing.
  • I'm hiding my thoughts about them — or any contact — from the people I trust.
  • I've started imagining or half-planning how a reunion would actually happen.
  • I've let them back in through "logistics" and the conversations are widening.

Euphoric recall is the mind playing you the highlight reel and hiding the rest. The warmth you're remembering was real — it just never stayed. That was the whole pattern.— The Undertow
03
When the current is strong

If You're Already Drifting

Catching it is the win. Here's what to do the moment you notice — screenshot this.

The moment I notice the pull:

  1. Name the stage out loud. "I'm in the mood drift / the tug-of-war / I'm reaching back." Naming it creates distance from it.
  2. Tell one safe person today. The pull feeds on secrecy. Breaking the silence breaks its hold.
  3. Go back to basics. Sleep, food, water, the meeting, the walk. The mood drift is reversed by care, not willpower.
  4. Re-read your reasons for leaving — in your own words, from when you were clear. (In The Anchor guide.)
  5. Don't act for 48 hours. The wave peaks and falls. You only have to outlast this one.
  6. If you already made contact — you have not failed. Returning is part of how most people eventually leave for good. Turn back toward your plan now.
04
For the strongest moments

Ride The Wave

An urge is a wave, not a wall. It rises, crests, and falls — usually within twenty to thirty minutes — whether or not you act on it. You only have to stay afloat through this one.

When the urge to reach out peaks:

  1. Notice and name it. "This is an urge. It will pass." Saying it puts space between you and the pull.
  2. Set a timer for 20 minutes. Promise yourself you'll do nothing until it rings. Urges almost always soften in that window.
  3. Move your body. Walk, shower, cold water on your face, step outside. Changing your physical state changes the wave.
  4. Call someone from your list — not them. A real voice shrinks the urge faster than anything else.
  5. When the timer rings, check again. Most of the time the wave has already dropped. If not — reset it. One wave at a time.
Reaching out once — or even going back — does not erase what you've learned. Most people who leave for good returned at least once first. The knowing you've built doesn't disappear. It comes with you.— Wuest & Merritt-Gray (1999)
